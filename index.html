01
Overview

A generation that has known nothing but war

Approximately 130 armed conflicts are active as of March 2026 — more than double the number recorded fifteen years ago. Six of the most dangerous involve the Middle East.

Analysis Desk · March 2026

The world has entered a new era of persistent, overlapping warfare. Unlike the Cold War's proxy conflicts or the post-9/11 decade of counterinsurgency, today's wars are simultaneously interstate, intrastate, and increasingly interlinked — feeding each other's supply chains of weapons, soldiers, and ideology.

The Council on Foreign Relations' 2026 Preventive Priorities Survey places six Middle Eastern conflicts in its Tier I and Tier II categories. Beyond the Middle East, a frozen-then-unfrozen war in Eastern Europe grinds on, a civil war in Sudan displaces millions unseen by the world's cameras, and a military junta in Myanmar continues to bomb its own civilians.

02
Ukraine

Russia's grinding war — an extraordinary price for minimal gains

After four years and over 1.2 million Russian casualties, the front line has barely moved. But the human cost to both nations is without modern precedent.

Analysis Desk · Sources: CSIS, Russia Matters / ISW, Al Jazeera

On February 24, 2026, Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine entered its fourth year. The Center for Strategic and International Studies estimates 1.2 million Russian military casualties — killed, wounded, and missing — by December 2025. Ukraine suffered between 500,000 and 600,000. Together, the war has consumed roughly two million soldiers.

"No major power has suffered anywhere near these numbers of casualties or fatalities in any war since World War II."

— Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS), January 2026

03
The 2026 Iran War

The night the Middle East changed — 900 strikes in 12 hours

On February 28, 2026, U.S. and Israeli forces launched the largest joint military operation in decades. The supreme leader of Iran was killed in the opening hours.

Analysis Desk · Sources: Britannica, CSIS, Al Jazeera

On the night of February 28, 2026, nearly 900 airstrikes hit Iranian targets in 12 hours — missiles, air defences, military infrastructure, and leadership bunkers. Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed within hours. The shock reverberated across financial markets, shipping lanes, and every capital in the world.

04
Sudan & Africa

The war the world forgot

Sudan's civil war — the world's fastest-growing humanitarian crisis — receives a fraction of the media coverage of conflicts with fewer casualties.

Analysis Desk · Sources: CFR 2026 Survey, ICRC

The CFR's 2026 survey rated Sudan's war as the single most likely conflict to escalate of all scenarios examined. Since April 2023, the Sudanese army has battled the Rapid Support Forces. Millions have been displaced; famine stalks entire regions. Yet it commands less than a fraction of the headlines devoted to Gaza or Ukraine.

Across the African continent, the Sahel region has become the new epicentre of jihadist insurgency, with Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, and Chad all experiencing sustained violence. The ICRC's 2026 humanitarian outlook describes Africa as facing a "compounding crisis" of conflict, climate, and food insecurity.

05
Analysis

Why is the world so much more violent?

A weakening of global institutions, the proliferation of cheap weapons, and the fragmentation of international order have created conditions where wars are easier to start and harder to end.

Analysis Desk

Several structural forces are converging. First, the post-Cold War "liberal peace" — the period of declining interstate conflict from 1990 to roughly 2010 — appears to have ended. Great powers are once again willing to use military force to revise borders.

Second, cheap technology has democratised lethal force. The use of commercial drones in Ukraine, Gaza, and Sudan has transformed battlefields. AI-enabled targeting, autonomous weapons, and cyber operations now run alongside kinetic conflict.

Third, the institutions designed to manage these tensions — the UN Security Council, formal alliance structures — are under profound strain. Russia's veto paralyses the Security Council on Ukraine. The US has grown less predictable. China remains ambiguous on nearly every conflict.

"Front lines today stretch across physical and digital worlds. The use of drones, AI, and cyber operations is accelerating — too often with devastating effects on civilians."

— ICRC Humanitarian Outlook 2026

The result is a world where wars last longer, involve more actors, and produce more displacement. Over 20 of today's conflicts have lasted more than two decades. There are now entire generations who have never known peace.
